What to expect, week by week

Phase 1
Pre-surgery preparation
2–4 weeks before

The weeks before surgery are your opportunity to build strength, prepare your home, and mentally prepare. Patients who complete prehabilitation exercises typically recover faster and require less pain medication post-surgery.

Goals

  • Build quad, glute, and hip flexor strength
  • Prepare your home for reduced mobility
  • Complete all medical clearances
  • Arrange transport and post-op support
  • Learn post-op exercises in advance

What to expect

  • Pre-op appointment with your surgical team
  • Blood tests and medical clearance checks
  • Pre-admission nursing assessment
  • Possible fasting instructions day before
  • Antiseptic wash the night before surgery
Recommended: quad sets, glute squeezes, mini squats See Phase 1 exercises →
Phase 2
Hospital stay
Days 1–3

You'll typically be in hospital for 1–3 days. The focus is on pain management, preventing complications, and taking your first steps. Most patients are up and walking with a walker within 24 hours of surgery.

Milestones

  • First steps with a walker (Day 1)
  • Sit to stand independently with aid
  • Walk 50–100 feet with walker
  • Manage stairs with railing (if required for home)
  • Pain managed with oral medication

What's normal

  • Significant pain and swelling around the hip
  • Bruising extending down the thigh or leg
  • Mild nausea from anesthesia
  • Fatigue — surgery is hard on the body
  • Catheter or drain tube (removed within 24 hrs)

Call your nurse immediately if you have chest pain, shortness of breath, severe calf pain or swelling (possible blood clot), or fever above 38°C (100.4°F).

Recommended: ankle pumps, heel slides, short walks See Phase 2 exercises →
Phase 3a
Home recovery — weeks 1 & 2
Weeks 1–2

The first two weeks at home are the most physically demanding. Rest is important, but so is gentle movement. You'll need help with meals, bathing, and transport. Most patients can walk short distances with a walker and are off the strongest pain medication by the end of week 2.

Milestones

  • Walking around the house with walker
  • Sleeping through the night (most nights)
  • Dressing lower body with aids (sock aid, reacher)
  • Managing stairs one step at a time
  • Wound healing, sutures or staples removed

What's normal

  • Ongoing swelling — elevate the leg when resting
  • Bruising continuing to spread and change colour
  • Clicking or clunking sensations in the joint
  • Interrupted sleep due to discomfort
  • Feeling emotionally low or frustrated — this is common

Contact your surgeon if you notice increasing redness, warmth or discharge at the wound, a fever, sudden worsening pain, or if the hip feels unstable or gives way.

Recommended: ankle pumps, heel slides, hip abduction See Phase 3 exercises →
Phase 3b
Building independence — weeks 3 to 6
Weeks 3–6

You'll begin transitioning from a walker to a cane, extending your walks, and regaining more independence with daily activities. Physical therapy visits become more structured and progressive during this period.

Milestones

  • Transition from walker to cane (around week 4–6)
  • Walking outdoors on flat surfaces
  • Showering independently (with shower chair)
  • Riding as a passenger in a car
  • Light household tasks while standing

What's normal

  • Swelling continuing, especially in the evening
  • Stiffness after sitting for extended periods
  • Fatigue after exercise — rest when needed
  • Muscle soreness from PT exercises
  • Increasing range of motion week by week
Recommended: seated knee extension, hip marching, hip extension See Phase 3 exercises →
Phase 4
Active rehabilitation
Weeks 6–12

With your surgeon's clearance (typically at the 6-week follow-up), you'll progress to more demanding exercises and activities. Most patients can drive again, return to desk work, and begin low-impact exercise like swimming or cycling during this phase.

Milestones

  • Walking without a cane for short distances
  • Driving again (automatic cars, non-operated leg ~6 wks)
  • Return to desk-based work
  • Swimming or stationary cycling
  • Climbing stairs with alternating feet

What's normal

  • Residual swelling, particularly after activity
  • Some stiffness in the morning
  • Occasional aching after longer walks
  • Hip still feeling "different" — this is normal
  • Gradual reduction in PT frequency
Recommended: step-ups, stationary bike, clamshells, partial lunges See Phase 4 exercises →
Phase 5
Return to full activity
Months 3–6

By 3–6 months most patients are walking without any aid, have returned to social activities, and have little to no pain. Full bone healing around the implant takes up to a year, so high-impact activities like running or contact sports require your surgeon's sign-off.

Milestones

  • Walking unlimited distances without aid
  • Return to physical work (varies by role)
  • Golf, hiking, doubles tennis (with clearance)
  • No significant pain with daily activities
  • Full range of motion largely restored

Long-term care

  • Annual check-ups with your orthopedic surgeon
  • X-rays every few years to monitor the implant
  • Maintain a healthy weight to reduce implant wear
  • Avoid high-impact running and jumping long-term
  • Alert future doctors that you have a hip implant

Seek urgent care at any stage if you develop sudden severe hip pain, a fever with hip pain, or if the leg becomes significantly shorter or rotated — these may indicate dislocation or late infection.
